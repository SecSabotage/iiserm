A Minor Inconvenience from the Student Body

**Archived for public record, as the official channels are experiencing "selective hearing."

Dear Professors,

We know your schedules are packed, but we kindly request a few minutes of your time to read this email. It is on the longer side, but it comes from a place of deep concern and pain.

You are likely tired of seeing emails from us, and honestly, we are exhausted, too. We never wanted to bother you repeatedly. It shouldn't have come to this, but our hands are tied, and we are feeling incredibly helpless. It feels as though our voices are entirely unheard, or simply going in one ear and out the other.

Many of us have been on this campus for a long time. We have faced issues before and navigated them through discussions, struggles, and negotiations. However, the current situation is beyond anything we could have imagined even a year ago. It is deeply frustrating, and our quality of life here is deteriorating in an accelerated manner. It often feels as though the administration treats us like prisoners or we are talking to walls. The Dean Students and the Dean Academics turn a deaf ear to us in almost every situation.

The truth is, it isn't just a single issue. It is the steady piling up of "little", as well as "big" things, a noticeable shift in attitude, and the growing manifestation of a deeply autocratic mentality.

For example, we know many of you were disappointed when the classroom blackboards were reduced in size without any prior consultation... practically, it causes real daily inconvenience. When these small, unconsulted decisions keep piling up, the entire campus environment goes on a downward curve.

Similarly, have you noticed how many trees and bushes have been cleared from the campus? ... Not only were there very few migratory birds this season, but the populations of resident birds, like peacocks and lapwings, have dropped drastically. Their nesting grounds have been wiped out simply so the fields can look "smooth and nice." ... And in exchange for all this, what do we have? A colorful, brand-new water fountain!

Now, let's come to the core issues we are currently facing.

1. Invasive Entry and Exit Protocols

Every time we enter or leave the campus, we are required to manually log our details in a register, including our hostel and room numbers. As adults, we find this level of surveillance both unnecessary and intrusive. Why must our exact living quarters be tracked and left visible in a notebook for anyone to see? Why must our movements be continuously monitored? Furthermore, from a purely logistical standpoint, this manual logging creates massive queues at the gate, proving the system is as inefficient as it is undignified.

2. Restrictive Gate Pass Policies and Moral Policing

We have been notified that gate passes will now only be issued for trips home or "emergencies." But how is an emergency defined, and by whom? After a week of extreme academic pressure, going out for a night show or taking a weekend trip is not an "emergency"; it is a basic right and a necessary mental health break for adults. Yet, we are being treated like inmates.

The implementation of this rule is even worse. Students are essentially forced to email the Dean Students to justify their "emergency" every time they try to get a Gate Pass. Worse still, caretakers have taken it upon themselves to deny passes even for students trying to go home, subjectively deciding that the student's reason isn't "urgent enough." In one deeply concerning instance, a caretaker demanded to know in writing who would be accompanying a female student on her return trip at night. This is blatant moral policing and an unacceptable overreach into our personal lives.

3. Unjustified CCTV and Caretaker Surveillance

CCTVs have recently been installed in the corridors of Hostels 3 and 4, with plans to expand this to the older hostels. Hostels are our personal living spaces. What could possibly justify constant surveillance in our homes, especially in buildings where rooms do not have attached washrooms? This completely strips away our basic privacy and dignity. How can we move or talk freely when a camera is continuously watching us?

Furthermore, caretakers are patrolling the hostel floors at random hours. There are instances of them intentionally moving quietly to avoid detection, eavesdropping, attempting to peek into rooms, and even looking through the glass vents above our doors. We cannot easily "prove" these incidents because eyewitness accounts are often dismissed, and we cannot take photographs of anyone without consent. We are adults, not a bunch of criminals. Even law enforcement requires a warrant for this level of intrusion, yet we are subjected to it daily in our own living quarters.

4. Failing Academic Infrastructure (Wi-Fi)

Student Wi-Fi barely works in our hostels, the Academic Blocks (ABs), and the Lecture Hall Complex (LHC). It goes without saying how critical a reliable internet connection is in a premier academic institution. It is a fundamental necessity for our research and coursework, yet we are constantly deprived of it, making our daily academic lives incredibly difficult.

We do want to genuinely acknowledge the Head of the Computer Centre, Prof. Satyajit Jena, and Prof. Shashi Bhushan Pandit, who have both been extremely receptive to our issues. We feel they have tried their absolute best to help us. However, it is glaringly obvious that the institute is not allocating enough resources to solve this critical infrastructure problem. It is profoundly disheartening to see immense funds poured into superficial campus "beautification" while the very backbone of our academic work is left underfunded and broken.

5. Arbitrary Internship Deadlines and Unclear Hostel Policies

Recently, the administration imposed a highly arbitrary and strict deadline of March 31st to submit declaration forms for in-house internships. This is unreasonable, considering almost every external summer program's results are declared in April. This is not about IISER Mohali being the last option. This is about students having options and choosing the most suitable one for them. For someone, a lab in a different institution might be best suited for their interests, but if they do not get there, they might want to work in a lab in IISER Mohali that is not exactly the same, but close enough. Someone might want to live in close proximity to their homes in the summer. Students were left stranded in uncertainty, and their options were severely limited.

Furthermore, these forms forced students to sign an agreement to abide by a "set of hostel instructions by the DSO", none of which were specified, even after repeated questioning during the open house. Also, there are multiple instances of one office putting the responsibility of answering our questions on the other office. And even in one office, different people provide different answers!

Adding to this, there are now new deadlines (June 30th for MS24 and MS25, and July 10th for other batches) for hostel accommodation, with the stated reason being repair and renovation work. These deadlines directly hinder the academic growth of younger batches by preventing them from completing highly advisable 8-week internships and also following their passions. When asked about the exact logistics of this renovation work during the open house, the DSO failed to give a satisfactory response. We are being unnecessarily forced to leave the campus for reasons that remain entirely opaque.

6. Substandard Food Hygiene and Unjustified Pricing

The hygiene of food outlets across the campus has been called out by students time and again. Multiple times, students saw rats roaming around in the kitchens. When RTIs were filed regarding this, they were met with unsatisfactory, one-word replies and zero follow-up action. Moreover, the food served in the LHC eatery is highly overpriced and economically unfeasible for most students. This arbitrary surge in prices has gone completely unchecked to the point of becoming unreasonable. Given the extremely limited food options across the campus, these compounding issues of poor hygiene and exorbitant pricing leave students with practically no choice.

7. Poor Maintenance of Basic Amenities (Drinking Water)

We should not need to emphasize the importance of a basic amenity like clean drinking water. The water coolers throughout the Academic Blocks are often unfit for use, and many were last inspected months ago. The situation is the same in the hostels. Some students are now forced to rely on bottled mineral water simply because they lack access to purified drinking water in their own living and working environment. There is only one RO water purifier in each hostel, and they are so poorly maintained that we frequently see fungus growing inside them.

8. Continuously Rising Fees and Financial Anxiety

It is a well-known fact that students pursuing basic sciences at IISER do not graduate into high-paying corporate jobs right away. Most of us go on to pursue academic research paths driven by passion, not immediate financial gain. Yet, the fee structure for newer batches is continuously rising to completely unrealistic levels. How can a science student, whose immediate future relies on modest stipends and research grants, be expected to afford such exorbitant fees? Many students are now forced to take out heavy educational loans just to study here. We came to this institute to focus on science, but instead, we are being burdened with immense financial anxiety before our careers have even begun. Education in a premier government science institute should be accessible to bright minds, not an overpriced luxury that plunges young researchers into debt. Education should not be for the few who can afford it. It should be for everyone, especially in a country that aims to be a world leader in Sciences and Technology.

9. Disproportionate Disciplinary Actions

Recently, a deeply concerning trend has emerged regarding DisCo (Disciplinary Committee) cases. Students committing minor, unintentional mistakes are being handed grave punishments that cause lasting academic and mental damage, even after issuing public apologies. It feels as though students are being penalized simply for expressing themselves, and any minor misstep can be weaponized against them if their views question or disagree with the administration. This has left a devastating impression on the younger batches. IISER Mohali was once known for institutional open dialogue; now, it has become a place where students are terrified to write even a simple email.

10. The Illusion of Student Representation

While we have an SRC to represent us in official discussions, it has been reduced to a body whose opinions are merely heard, but never listened to. The SRC has expressed dissent regarding numerous recent administrative decisions, but it is all in vain because the administration practices selective hearing, only acknowledging student opinions that align with their own agendas. For instance, regarding the camera issue, 596 student signatories submitted an objection statement, yet the issue was never even considered for discussion. The DSO flatly refused to talk about it during the open house.

There is a glaring contradiction here: on one hand, our respected Director encourages us to ask questions and strive to become an actively thinking scientific workforce for the country. On the other hand, we are forced to blindly accept blatantly questionable rules. Decisions affecting students are never genuinely discussed with us. The DSO has even stopped engaging with the student body via email, leaving long threads pleading for answers met with deafening silence. We urgently need broader student representation and real decision-making power over the issues that dictate our daily lives.

We understand that this email is long. But honestly, it isn't long enough to describe all the seemingly "little" and glaringly big things that are slowly deteriorating our lives and academics. We came here with a dream: to study, to live, to socialize, to become independent adults, and most importantly, to build a scientific temperament. But we can only do that when the administration makes student well-being a priority.

There are plenty of academic challenges for us to focus on: updating age-old lab equipment, providing proper Wifi, updating projectors in classrooms, installing full-sized boards, building more Academic Blocks, integrating modern ideas, and whatnot. The administration should be making our daily lives easier, not harder. That means no unjustified surveillance, no policing of our personal lives, and no treating us like criminals or prisoners. We must live freely, talk freely, and move freely. Science thrives only when our minds are free.

We acknowledge that no system is perfect, and individuals who commit genuine wrongs should face consequences. However, collective punishment is never the answer. If you have a headache, you don't cut off the head; otherwise, we will simply continue down an infinite downward spiral. You do not want that, and neither do we.

That is why we are writing to you today: to help you truly feel what we are going through. Even if each incident feels small in isolation, please try to understand the crushing weight of them piling up together. And trust us, these issues are not small. We used to take immense pride in being students of IISER Mohali. Please help us retain that pride.

Thank you very much for your time and patience.

Sincerely,

Concerned Students of IISER Mohali
